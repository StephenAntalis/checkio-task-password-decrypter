Jack is the primary network administrator at a major law firm. Like most IT professionals, he loves the outdoors and goes hiking nearly every chance he gets. Three days ago he left the office for a week long hiking excursion in the Swiss Alps and left a single flash drive in your care in case anything went wrong in his absence.

The law firm regularly hires security experts to try to hack into your firm’s networks in order to expose any vulnerabilities that a hacker could exploit. This morning one of the hackers exposed a minor hole on one of the networks that, if properly exploited, could allow hackers to elevate the privileges of normal users into super users. Your company quickly came up with a patch that would fix the problem however this network was specifically designed so only a command from your company’s primary network administrator could initiate network updates.

Unfortunately, this means only Jack’s account is capable of initiating the patch. Since this exploit is capable of compromising all data on that particular network, the company has informed you that you must immedately install the patch onto the exposed network. Fortunately you know that his flash drive contains a means of extracting his primary network password. You opened the drive and immediately recognize that both of the on the drive are encrypted using AES encryption. On a hunch, you attempt to decrypt both of the files using your personal private key. One of the files becomes instantly readable but the other remains unintelligible garbage. The legible document states:

"If you are reading this, I am currently indisposed. The second enclosed document on this drive is encrypted using my own personal brand of encryption. In order to obtain all of my passwords, you must run it through my own unique band of decryption software.

In order to decrypt the passwords, you must create a program capable of taking in a single string as input. If the string has an even number of characters, start with the first and last character, then second and second to last character, moving inwards, take their ascii values, convert them to base 2 and do XOR on the last 6 binary numbers of each pair. The rest of the numbers are ANDed together. If there is an odd number of characters, then the middle letter will have its last 6 characters inverted (all 1s to 0s and all 0s to 1). Then these numbers will be converted back to their base 10 ascii characters and output the message in the order of outside to inside."

Input: String

Output: String
